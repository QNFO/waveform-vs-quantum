# T3.1: Formalizing the Quantum Factoring Claim Gap

## 1. The Raw Claim

Let $C$ be the popular claim:

> **C**: *"Quantum computers provide a practical advantage over classical computers for integer factorization."*

This is the claim that animates quantum computing investment, post-quantum cryptography migration, and NIST standardization. It is rarely stated precisely.

---

## 2. Decomposing C into Logical Atoms

Express $C$ as the conjunction of nine premises $P_1$ through $P_9$:

$$
C \equiv P_1 \wedge P_2 \wedge P_3 \wedge P_4 \wedge P_5 \wedge P_6 \wedge P_7 \wedge P_8 \wedge P_9
$$

| Premise | Statement | Status |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| $P_1$ | FACTORING $\in$ BQP | **Theorem** — Shor (1994) |
| $P_2$ | Shor's algorithm succeeds with probability ≥ 1/3 per trial on any composite $N$ | **Theorem** — Shor (1994), strengthened by subsequent analysis |
| $P_3$ | QFT on $n$ qubits requires $O(n^2)$ gates | **Theorem** — Coppersmith (1994), approximate QFT $O(n \log n)$ |
| $P_4$ | Shor's circuit for factoring $n$-bit integers requires $O(n^2 \log n \log \log n)$ gates | **Theorem** — modular exponentiation via schoolbook multiplication; reduced to $\widetilde{O}(n^2)$ with fast multiplication |
| $P_5$ | FACTORING $\notin$ BPP | **Conjecture** — equivalent to classical hardness of factoring |
| $P_6$ | Fault-tolerant quantum computers can be built at the scale required | **Empirical/Engineering** — no known principle forbids it, but has not been demonstrated |
| $P_7$ | The overhead of quantum error correction (surface codes: $O(d^2)$ physical qubits per logical qubit) does not eliminate asymptotic advantage | **Conditional theorem** — yes for $d \sim O(\log N)$; the constant factor (~1,000× to 10,000×) is the practical concern |
| $P_8$ | Quantum gate fidelities are sufficient to run Shor's algorithm at the target $N$ with acceptable error | **Empirical** — current best fidelities ~99.9% per 2-qubit gate; threshold theorem requires >99.0%–99.9% depending on code |
| $P_9$ | The quantum resource requirements (qubits × gates) are achievable before classical factoring improvements or alternative attacks make the target $N$ irrelevant | **Speculative** — depends on timeline, RSA key size evolution, algorithmic progress on both sides |

---

## 3. Status Classification

### Theorems ($P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$)
- **$P_1$** is the seminal result of Shor (1994): there exists a polynomial-time quantum algorithm (in BQP) that, given a composite integer $N$, outputs a non-trivial factor with bounded-error probability. This is a theorem — it follows from the quantum circuit model axioms and standard complexity theory.
- **$P_2$** is the success probability analysis from Shor's original paper, refined by subsequent work (Ekert & Jozsa 1996, Nielsen & Chuang §5.3). For RSA semiprimes, $P(\text{success}) \geq 1/2$ per trial; for general $N$, it is at least $P(\text{success}) \geq 1 - 2^{-k+1}$ where $k$ is the number of distinct odd prime factors.
- **$P_3$** is a standard result about the Quantum Fourier Transform circuit.
- **$P_4$** follows from $P_3$ plus the modular exponentiation construction using classical reversible arithmetic.

### Conjecture ($P_5$)
- **$P_5$** is the statement that FACTORING is not in BPP (bounded-error probabilistic polynomial time). This is widely believed but unproven. It is *at least as strong as* P $\neq$ NP (since FACTORING $\in$ NP $\cap$ coNP, and FACTORING $\notin$ BPP would imply P $\neq$ NP if we believe P = BPP). More precisely, $P_5$ is a **complexity separation conjecture** — stronger than P $\neq$ NP in that it asserts a specific problem is not in BPP.

### Empirical/Engineering ($P_6, P_7, P_8, P_9$)
- These are not statements of mathematics but of physical realizability. $P_7$ has a conditional theorem structure but the practical constants dominate.
- $P_9$ is the most tenuous — it's a statement about the intersection of two evolving technology curves.

---

## 4. When Is C Meaningful?

$C$ implies a conjunction of two complexity-theoretic statements for it to represent a genuine *advantage*:

$$
C \implies (\text{FACTORING} \in \text{BQP}) \wedge (\text{FACTORING} \notin \text{BPP})
$$

Both must hold:

1. **FACTORING $\in$ BQP** — quantum computers *can* do it efficiently. This is proved ($P_1$).
2. **FACTORING $\notin$ BPP** — classical computers *cannot* do it efficiently. This is conjectured ($P_5$).

If FACTORING $\in$ BPP, then $P_5$ is false and $C$ is false (classical computers can match quantum performance), even though FACTORING $\in$ BQP remains true.

---

## 5. The Complexity Separation Required

### 5.1 The Hierarchy

Consider the possible states of knowledge:

| Scenario | FACTORING $\in$ BQP? | FACTORING $\in$ BPP? | C true? | Significance |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|---------|--------------|
| (i) | Yes | No | **Yes** | Quantum advantage exists |
| (ii) | Yes | Yes | **No** | Both can factor; no advantage |
| (iii) | No | No | **No** | No one can factor (unlikely at large scale, e.g., GNFS for very large $N$) |
| (iv) | No | Yes | **No** | Contradicts $P_1$; ruled out by Shor's theorem |

### 5.2 The Separation Equivalent

$C$ being true requires:

$$
\text{BQP} \not\subseteq \text{BPP} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{FACTORING} \in \text{BQP} \setminus \text{BPP}
$$

This is equivalent to:

> **$\text{BPP} \subsetneq \text{BQP}$** (BQP is strictly larger than BPP) *and* FACTORING witnesses the separation.

Note: FACTORING is not known to be BQP-complete (that would require proving a quantum reduction from arbitrary BQP problems to FACTORING). The BQP-complete problems currently known are the Approximate QFT trace estimation and related unitary approximation problems (Aharonov et al. 2007, Janzing & Wocjan 2007).

### 5.3 Weakest Separation That Makes C True

The weakest complexity assumption that makes $C$ true is:

> **FACTORING $\notin$ BPP** alone.

We do *not* need to prove BPP $\subsetneq$ BQP in general — we only need to prove that this one specific problem (FACTORING) is not in BPP. This is a **problem-specific separation**, not a class separation.

However, demonstrating FACTORING $\notin$ BPP would *imply* BPP $\subsetneq$ BQP (since FACTORING $\in$ BQP is already proven), so the problem-specific separation would cascade to a class separation as a corollary.

---

## 6. Intermediate Possibilities

### 6.1 What if FACTORING $\in$ BQP $\cap$ BPP?

Then $C$ is false. But this scenario would mean:
- Factoring is classically easy (in BPP).
- RSA, Diffie-Hellman, elliptic curve cryptography (via reductions) are all broken classically.
- The entire post-quantum cryptography migration would be unnecessary (since classical computers already break the systems).
- This would be a **far more disruptive** result than Shor's algorithm itself — it would mean the security of deployed public-key crypto was always illusory, independent of quantum computing.

### 6.2 What if FACTORING is *strictly harder* than BPP but easier than NP-complete?

This is the consensus view: FACTORING is believed to be in NP $\cap$ coNP but not in P (or BPP), and not NP-complete (unless the polynomial hierarchy collapses). This is the "intermediate" complexity regime, similar to Graph Isomorphism and lattice problems.

### 6.3 What if Shor's algorithm is not practically scalable ($P_7$ or $P_8$ fail)?

If $P_6$–$P_9$ fail but $P_1$–$P_4$ hold, then FACTORING $\in$ BQP *in principle* but not *in practice*. This is the "asymptotic vs. concrete" gap that much of the debate centers on. The claim $C$ as stated includes "practical advantage," so empirical failure of $P_6$–$P_9$ would falsify $C$ even if the asymptotic theorems stand.

---

## 7. Summary

| Component | Status | Certainty |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| FACTORING $\in$ BQP | Theorem (Shor 1994) | Certain (in the quantum circuit model) |
| FACTORING $\notin$ BPP | Conjecture | Unknown; implied by but weaker than BPP $\subsetneq$ BQP |
| Quantum error correction viability | Conditional theorem | Algebraically proven; constant factors dominate practical feasibility |
| Gate fidelity sufficiency | Engineering target | Currently below threshold for factoring cryptographically relevant $N$ |
| Timeline feasibility | Speculation | Contested |

**The "claim gap"**: The popular claim $C$ is a conjunction of one theorem, one conjecture, two conditional theorems, and four engineering/empirical statements. Only $P_1$–$P_4$ are settled. The truth of $C$ depends on $P_5$ (a deep complexity conjecture) and $P_6$–$P_9$ (engineering realizability). The weakest formal condition that makes $C$ true is FACTORING $\notin$ BPP, but even this modest conjecture remains unproven after decades of effort.
