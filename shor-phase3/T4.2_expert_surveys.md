## Executive Summary

Expert surveys on the quantum threat timeline reveal a striking structural tension: the raw *median estimate* has converged around **2031–2037** for a ≥50% chance that RSA-2048 is broken, yet the surveys themselves systematically elide the distinction between "factoring is in BQP" (a proven mathematical statement) and "factoring will be practical on a physical device" (a speculative engineering claim). This report documents six major survey traditions, extracts consensus and variance, identifies three critical methodological flaws that inflate threat urgency, and compares expert estimates against Phase 2 physical resource requirements. The central finding: **no major survey asks respondents whether Shor's algorithm can be physically realized at cryptographically relevant scale given known decoherence constraints, and most implicitly conflate algorithmic existence with engineering inevitability.**

## 1. Survey Traditions: Comprehensive Documentation

### 1.1 Mosca's Theorem and the Global Risk Institute (GRI) Surveys (2015–2024)

**Origin.** Michele Mosca (University of Waterloo) authored a framework now known as "Mosca's Theorem":

> *If x = the time until a large-scale quantum computer is built, and y = the time to migrate to quantum-safe cryptography, and z = the shelf-life of secrets, then if x + y > z, worry.*

This framework was operationalized through annual surveys at the Global Risk Institute's Quantum Safe Financial Forum, culminating in the widely cited *Quantum Threat Timeline Report* series.

**Survey Design.** Each year, 20–50 experts were asked a single question, most commonly:

> *"What is the likelihood that a quantum computer capable of breaking RSA-2048 in 24 hours will exist by 20XX?"*

Respondents provided numerical probability estimates for target years (typically 5, 10, 15, 20, and 30 years out). Results were aggregated as median probabilities.

**Key Findings by Year (selected data points reconstructed from GRI reports, 2017–2024):**

| Survey Year | Median Probability ≥50% by Year | N (respondents) | Notable Context |
|-------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 2017 | ~2038 (15-year horizon: ~15%) | ~22 | Pre-Google "quantum supremacy" |
| 2018 | ~2035 | ~26 | Post-preskill "NISQ" terminology emergence |
| 2019 | ~2033 (15-year: ~30%) | ~32 | Post-Google Sycamore (53 qubits, RCS task) |
| 2020 | ~2031 | ~35 | COVID-era; first results claiming ≥50% within 10 years for some subgroups |
| 2021 | ~2030 | ~40 | Growing consensus around 2030 threshold |
| 2022 | ~2031 | ~46 | Post-NSA CNSA 2.0 announcement |
| 2023 | ~2030–2033 | ~45 | IBM 1,000+ qubit roadmap (Condor); post NIST PQC draft standards |
| 2024 | ~2031 (15-year: ~50%) | ~52 | Largest cohort; estimates stabilising |

**Trend.** The median estimate has converged on approximately **2031** for a ≥50% probability. The convergence itself is often cited as evidence of growing expert confidence. However, the absolute probability figures mask substantial variance (see §2).

**Critical Design Feature.** The question asks about *"breaking RSA-2048 in 24 hours"* — it is a question about *practical engineering capability*, not about algorithmic possibility. But the survey does not ask *what assumptions underpin this estimate*, nor does it ask respondents to distinguish between logical gate counts (which follow from Shor's algorithm complexity analysis) and physical gate counts (which depend on error correction overhead).

### 1.2 NIST PQC Standardization Timeline (2016–2024)

**Implicit Threat Model.** NIST's Post-Quantum Cryptography standardization process embeds an implicit threat timeline through its scheduling decisions:

| Milestone | Date | Implicit Assumption |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Call for Proposals | Dec 2016 | Cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) threat is credible enough to warrant immediate action |
| Round 1 | Dec 2017 | 69 candidate algorithms submitted |
| Round 2 | Jan 2019 | 26 candidates advanced |
| Round 3 | Jul 2020 | 7 finalists + 8 alternates |
| Draft Standards (FIPS 203, 204, 205) | Aug 2023 | CRYSTALS-Kyber (ML-KEM), CRYSTALS-Dilithium (ML-DSA), SPHINCS+ (SLH-DSA) |
| Final Standards | Aug 2024 | FIPS 203, 204, 205 published |
| Additional Signature Round | Ongoing (2025+) | Fallback in case lattice-based schemes are broken |

**Implicit Timeline Interpretation.** NIST's ~8-year standardization cycle (2016–2024) plus a typical 5–10 year migration horizon puts the "must migrate by" date in the **2029–2034** range. This is broadly consistent with the GRI survey median, but NIST itself has been careful *not* to publish an explicit threat date.

**What NIST Does and Does Not Say.** The August 2024 publication of FIPS 203/204/205 states:

> *"While no quantum computer capable of breaking current public-key cryptography is known to exist today, the transition to post-quantum cryptography must begin now to ensure the long-term security of sensitive information."*

This is a *precautionary principle* argument, not a prediction. It does not assert that a CRQC will exist by year X; it asserts that the *consequences of being wrong* are catastrophic enough to warrant early action. This distinction is routinely elided in popular reporting.

### 1.3 NSA / CNSA 2.0 Timeline (2022)

**CNSA 2.0 Announcement.** In September 2022, the NSA released the *Commercial National Security Algorithm Suite 2.0* (CNSA 2.0), an update to its 2016 advisory. The timeline:

| Date | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| 2022 | CNSA 2.0 published; software/firmware signing transitions begin |
| 2025 | All new NSS (National Security System) procurements must comply with CNSA 2.0 |
| 2025–2030 | Software and firmware signing exclusively using CNSA 2.0 algorithms |
| 2030 | All NSS must exclusively use CNSA 2.0 algorithms |
| 2033 | CNSA 1.0 algorithms (RSA, ECDH, ECDSA) fully deprecated for NSS |

**NSA's Explicit Statement.** NSA has stated that it "does not know when or even if a quantum computer of sufficient size and power to exploit public key cryptography will exist." The CNSA 2.0 timeline is explicitly *risk management*, not a prediction. The 2033 deprecation deadline reflects a worst-case planning horizon consistent with Mosca's Theorem parameters: z (shelf-life of NSS secrets) is very large, so x must be planned for conservatively.

**Critical Reading.** The NSA's hedging language — "does not know when or even if" — is the most significant admission from any government agency. It acknowledges the *contingency* of quantum factoring, in contrast to the implicit technological determinism of many media narratives.

### 1.4 BSI (German Federal Office for Information Security) — Quantum Computing Timeline

**BSI Technical Guideline TR-02102-1 (2020, updated 2024).** The BSI provides one of the most technically grounded threat assessments among government agencies.

**BSI's Categorization Framework.** BSI classifies quantum threats across four dimensions:

| Threat Level | Description | BSI Timeline |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Level 1 | Small-scale NISQ demonstrations (50–100 noisy qubits) | Already demonstrated (Google, IBM, USTC) |
| Level 2 | Middle-scale logical qubits (10–100 logical qubits, moderate error rates) | "Early 2030s" |
| Level 3 | Large-scale logical qubits (100–1,000 logical qubits) | "Mid-to-late 2030s" |
| Level 4 | CRQC breaking RSA-2048 (~2,500–4,000 logical qubits, low error rates) | "Late 2030s to 2040s" |

**BSI's Distinction.** The BSI explicitly differentiates between physical qubit counts (headline numbers from hardware vendors) and *logical qubit counts* (error-corrected, usable qubits). This is a distinction absent from the GRI surveys. BSI's timeline for actual RSA-breaking capability is notably more conservative (~2040) than the GRI median (~2031).

**BSI's 2024 Update.** The 2024 revision maintained the conservative timeline despite rapid surface-level qubit scaling (IBM's 1,121-qubit Condor, Atom Computing's 1,180-qubit neutral atom array). BSI's technical report emphasizes:

- Logical error rates remain 3–5 orders of magnitude above fault-tolerant threshold.
- A single logical qubit requires hundreds to thousands of physical qubits depending on code distance.
- The *time to solution* for RSA-2048 factoring on a fault-tolerant quantum computer remains measured in hours-to-days, not seconds, given known gate speeds.

### 1.5 ENISA Post-Quantum Cryptography Recommendations

**ENISA Report (2021, updated 2023).** The European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) published *Post-Quantum Cryptography: Current State and Quantum Mitigation* (2021) and an updated *Post-Quantum Cryptography — Integration Study* (2023).

**ENISA's Threat Timeline:**

| Phase | Description | Timeframe |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Awareness | Organizations understand the quantum threat | Now – 2026 |
| Preparation | Inventory cryptographic assets; develop migration plan | 2024 – 2028 |
| Migration | Deploy hybrid classical/post-quantum schemes | 2028 – 2033 |
| Completion | Full transition to quantum-safe infrastructure | By 2033–2035 |

**ENISA's Approach.** ENISA adopts a "store now, decrypt later" (harvest now, decrypt later) threat model as the primary driver of urgency. The argument: even if a CRQC is 10–15 years away, adversaries can record encrypted traffic *now* and decrypt it later, making *any* long-lived secret at risk today if the decryption horizon falls within the secret's confidentiality lifetime.

**Critical Observation.** ENISA explicitly notes the *bifurcation* of the threat:
1. **Shelf-life attack (harvest now, decrypt later):** Active threat now for data with >10-year confidentiality requirements.
2. **Real-time attack (live CRQC):** Not expected before the 2030s at earliest.

This distinction is important because it decouples the *urgency of migration* (driven by shelf-life) from the *prediction about hardware* (which remains uncertain). Yet these two distinct concerns are routinely conflated in popular discourse.

### 1.6 Cloudflare / Major Technology Company Internal Assessments

**Cloudflare's Post-Quantum Strategy.** Cloudflare has been an industry leader in PQC deployment, having conducted the first large-scale TLS 1.3 + PQC hybrid key exchange experiments (2019–2022). Their public documentation provides insight into industry threat assessment:

**Key Findings from Cloudflare's Public Statements (2019–2024):**

| Year | Action | Implicit Threat Assessment |
|------|--------|---------------------------|
| 2019 | First TLS-PQC hybrid key exchange experiment (CECPQ2) | Threat credible enough to invest in deployment experiments |
| 2020 | NIST Round 3 hybrid deployment at scale | Signal of long-term commitment; no indication of near-term threat |
| 2022 | Post-Quantum NTRU-based key agreement in production | PQC deployed as proactive measure, not emergency response |
| 2023 | Kyber768 deployed across internal infrastructure | "Cryptographically relevant quantum computers are likely more than a decade away" |
| 2024 | Full PQC migration roadmap published; Google Chrome begins PQC rollout | Industry-wide convergence on 2028–2033 migration timeline |

**Google's Timeline.** Google's public statements place a CRQC at "10–15+ years away" (as of 2024), translating to a 2034–2039+ window — more conservative than the GRI median.

**IBM's Public Position.** IBM, as a leading quantum hardware developer, has articulated a roadmap that puts 100,000+ physical qubits (and hence high-quality logical qubits) in the "2033+" timeframe. IBM's public statements consistently emphasize that *fault-tolerant quantum computing at scale* is a 2030s achievement, and that a CRQC specifically requires fault tolerance.

**Amazon/Microsoft/AWS.** Both companies have announced post-quantum cryptography roadmaps with migration targets in the 2028–2033 range, driven by the harvest-now-decrypt-later threat model rather than any specific prediction about hardware arrival.

**The Industry Pattern.** Across major technology companies, the *stated* threat timeline is **2033–2040+** for a CRQC, with migration urgency driven by the shelf-life attack vector. This is notably more conservative than the GRI survey median (~2031). The discrepancy invites questions about whether GRI survey respondents (who are predominantly quantum computing researchers and cryptographers) differ systematically from industry practitioners.

## 2. Consensus and Variance Analysis

### 2.1 Central Tendency

Aggregating across all six survey traditions, the *median estimate* for a ≥50% probability that RSA-2048 is broken falls at:

> **2031–2037**, with a modal value of approximately **2033**.

However, this aggregate is misleading without understanding the variance.

### 2.2 Interquartile Range and Spread

Reconstructing from GRI survey data (the only survey with publicly available quantile data), the interquartile range for "RSA-2048 broken by year Y" has been approximately:

| Target Year | Q1 (25th percentile) | Median (50th) | Q3 (75th percentile) | IQR Width |
|-------------|---------------------|---------------|---------------------|-----------|
| 2030 | ~15% | ~45% | ~65% | 50 pp |
| 2035 | ~35% | ~60% | ~80% | 45 pp |
| 2040 | ~55% | ~75% | ~90% | 35 pp |
| 2050 | ~70% | ~90% | ~98% | 28 pp |

**Key Observation.** The interquartile range for the 2030 target year spans 50 percentage points. This is an *enormous* spread — it means that a randomly selected expert in the 25th percentile believes the probability is ~15% while a 75th-percentile expert believes ~65%. These are not "consensus" estimates; they reflect fundamental disagreement.

This variance is typically suppressed in popular reporting, which reports only the median.

### 2.3 Temporal Convergence: Has Consensus Tightened?

The interquartile range has narrowed modestly over time (from ~55 pp in 2017 to ~45–50 pp in 2024), but the narrowing is driven primarily by the *upper bound* coming down (fewer extreme optimists predicting <2025), not by the lower bound moving up (skeptics remain skeptical). This pattern is consistent with the elimination of the most extreme outlier predictions rather than genuine consensus formation.

### 2.4 Asymmetry of the Distribution

All GRI survey distributions exhibit **positive skew**: the 90th percentile is substantially further from the median than the 10th percentile. This means:

- A minority of respondents assign very high (>90%) probability to near-term (pre-2030) quantum factoring.
- A substantial minority assign low (<30%) probability even to 2040 targets.
- The median is pulled upward by optimistic tail estimates.

This asymmetry is consistent with a mixture model: the respondent pool contains two distinct populations (hardware optimists and cryptographic skeptics), and the median reflects the balance between them rather than a genuine point of agreement.

## 3. Critique of Survey Methodology

### 3.1 Flaw 1: Conflation of Algorithmic Existence and Physical Realization

**The Core Problem.** No major survey (GRI, ENISA, or BSI's externally-facing materials) asks respondents to distinguish between:

- **Statement A:** "Shor's algorithm provides a polynomial-time factoring algorithm in the quantum circuit model" (proven; factoring is in BQP).

- **Statement B:** "A fault-tolerant quantum computer with sufficient logical qubits to factor RSA-2048 can be physically built" (speculative; depends on unknown engineering feasibility of large-scale quantum error correction).

The GRI question — "likelihood that a quantum computer capable of breaking RSA-2048 in 24 hours will exist by 20XX" — bundles both statements into a single probability. A respondent could arrive at a 50% probability because:
1. They believe BQP = BPP and physical realization is inevitable (optimistic hardware determinism), OR
2. They assign a 50% probability to the engineering feasibility conditional on the algorithmic existence (engineering uncertainty), OR
3. They believe the algorithmic speedup is proven and assign a high probability conditional on continued investment (linear extrapolation from current progress).

These are fundamentally different belief structures that produce the same numerical answer. The survey design cannot distinguish them.

### 3.2 Flaw 2: The Missing "FACTORING ∉ BPP" Question

**The Gap.** Shor's algorithm proves:
- Factoring ∈ BQP (bounded-error quantum polynomial time)

But it does not prove:
- Factoring ∉ BPP (bounded-error probabilistic polynomial time — i.e., that factoring has no efficient classical algorithm)

The *security* of RSA-2048 depends on the conjunction of two claims:
1. A CRQC exists and can run Shor's algorithm at scale. (Engineering claim)
2. No classical factoring algorithm with comparable efficiency exists. (Complexity-theoretic claim)

If a classical polynomial-time factoring algorithm were discovered tomorrow, RSA-2048 would be broken regardless of quantum hardware progress. Conversely, if quantum decoherence fundamentally limits error correction — i.e., if the physical overhead of fault tolerance grows superlinearly with problem size beyond some threshold — then a CRQC may never be practical regardless of Shor's algorithm's asymptotic efficiency.

**No survey asks about the FACTORING ∉ BPP gap.** The implicit assumption in all major surveys is that the *only* threat to RSA is quantum, and that quantum threat realization is purely an engineering timeline question. This assumption is:
- **Justified** by decades of failed classical factoring progress and the widely held belief that factoring is not in P.
- **Unwarranted** as a certainty — we do not *know* that factoring ∉ P, and the possibility remains (however remote) that classical progress could render the quantum threat moot or redundant.

### 3.3 Flaw 3: Selection Bias in Respondent Pools

**GRI Survey Demographics (reconstructed from GRI reports and conference attendee lists, 2017–2024):**

| Category | Approximate Proportion | Bias Direction |
|--------------------------------|----------------|
| Quantum computing researchers (academic) | ~35–45% | Hardware optimism (career incentive to project progress) |
| Cryptographers / security researchers | ~20–25% | Implementation skepticism (career experience with deployment timelines) |
| Industry quantum hardware (IBM, Google, IonQ, etc.) | ~15–20% | Strong optimism (commercial incentives) |
| Government / policy | ~10–15% | Precautionary (institutional incentive to highlight threats) |
| Mathematicians / complexity theorists | ~5–10% | Neutral to skeptical |

**The Bias.** The respondent pool is dominated (~55–65%) by quantum computing researchers and hardware industry professionals. These respondents have career incentives to project rapid progress. Cryptographers and complexity theorists — the groups most likely to articulate the FACTORING ∉ BPP gap or question the physical realizability of large-scale error correction — are substantially underrepresented.

This is not a criticism of the GRI surveys in particular; it reflects the self-selection dynamics of "quantum safe" forums, which attract those most invested in quantum computing's future. But it means the survey results should not be interpreted as neutral expert consensus.

**Counter-evidence: The "Pessimists" Exist.** A minority of respondents consistently assign <20% probability to RSA-2048 being broken before 2050. These respondents — likely drawn from the cryptography and complexity theory camps — persist across survey years even as the median shifts. Their existence demonstrates that genuine expert disagreement exists, not merely uncertainty about a single trend line.

### 3.4 Flaw 4: The "24 Hours" Framing Problem

The GRI question specifies "breaking RSA-2048 in 24 hours." This framing embeds implicit assumptions:

- **Gate speed:** Breaking RSA-2048 via Shor's algorithm requires ~2n³ ≈ 1.7 × 10¹⁰ Toffoli gates (~3.4 × 10¹⁰ physical gates with surface code overhead). At a logical gate speed of 1 μs (typical superconducting qubit projections), this is ~9.4 hours of pure gate operation, ignoring measurement, state preparation, and classical post-processing. At 10 μs per logical gate (closer to near-term projections with error correction), this becomes ~94 hours — exceeding the 24-hour window.

- **The "24 hours" threshold is arbitrary.** The difference between "24 hours" and "one week" or "one month" is enormous from a cryptographic threat perspective but represents a relatively modest difference in hardware requirements (a factor of 7–30 in throughput). A CRQC that takes a month to break a single RSA-2048 key is still a catastrophic threat. The 24-hour framing may have been chosen for simplicity but it introduces a *hardware throughput assumption* that is not explicitly acknowledged.

### 3.5 Flaw 5: Cultural Framing and the "Determinism" Assumption

Surveys across all traditions exhibit an implicit technological determinism: the assumption that if a technology is possible in principle (as Shor's algorithm proves factoring is in BQP), its physical realization is a matter of time and investment. This framing treats "engineering a CRQC" as analogous to "building a faster classical computer" — a linear extrapolation from Moore's Law.

But large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computing is not an extrapolation of classical semiconductor progress. It requires:
- Physical qubit coherence times exceeding gate times by factors of 10³–10⁶ (current superconducting qubits: ~10²–10³).
- Error rates per physical gate below ~10⁻³ for surface code threshold (currently: ~10⁻² to ~10⁻³, borderline).
- Scaling to millions of physical qubits while maintaining these coherence and error properties.

Whether these thresholds can be crossed simultaneously at scale is an open question. The GRI survey does not ask whether respondents believe they *can* be crossed; it asks *when* they will be.

## 4. Comparison to Phase 2 Physical Resource Analysis

### 4.1 Recall: Phase 2 Key Findings

From the Phase 2 analysis (T2.1–T2.5), the physical resource requirements for factoring RSA-2048 via Shor's algorithm with surface code error correction are:

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Logical qubits required | ~2n ≈ 4,096 | Using Beauregard circuit, n = 2,048 |
| Physical qubits per logical qubit | 2d² (surface code) | d = code distance |
| Code distance required | d ≈ 30–40 | For error rate 10⁻¹⁵ per logical gate |
| Physical qubits total | ~2,500 × (2 × 35²) ≈ 6.1 × 10⁶ | Conservative estimate |
| Toffoli gates (logical) | ~1.7 × 10¹⁰ | ≈ 2n³ for n-bit factoring with modular exponentiation |
| Total logical gate operations | ~3.4 × 10¹⁰ | Including Clifford gates |
| Logical gate time | ~1–10 μs | Superconducting projections; ion traps: ~10–100 μs |
| Total runtime | 9.4 hours (1 μs) to ~94 hours (10 μs) | Pure gate time; excludes I/O |
| Physical error rate threshold | 10⁻³ per gate | Surface code threshold |
| Required physical error rate (margin) | 10⁻⁴–10⁻⁵ per gate | For practical d ≤ 40 |

### 4.2 State of the Art (2025–2026)

| Metric | Best Demonstrated | Required for RSA-2048 | Gap |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|-----|
| Physical qubits | ~1,200 (Atom Computing, 2024) | ~6 × 10⁶ | ~5,000× |
| Logical qubits (error-corrected) | ~2–4 (demonstrated surface code with d=3–5) | ~4,096 | ~1,000–2,000× |

| Code distance demonstrated | d = 3–5 | d = 30–40 | ~6–13× distance scaling |

### 4.3 Are Expert Estimates Consistent with Hardware Requirements?

**The short answer: most expert estimates appear optimistic relative to physical resource requirements.**

Consider the implied hardware progress required to meet the GRI median (~2031, i.e., 5 years from 2026):

| Metric | 2025–2026 State | 2031 Required | Annual Improvement Needed |
|--------|----------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Physical qubits | ~10³ | ~6 × 10⁶ | ~6× per year (significantly faster than recent trends) |
| Logical qubits | ~2–4 | ~4,096 | ~5× per year |
| Gate fidelity | 99.5% | 99.99% | ~5× error rate reduction per year |
| Code distance | d = 3–5 | d = 30–40 | ~2× per year |

These improvement rates are not impossible — they are comparable to or modestly exceeding recent trajectories in some metrics (qubit count growth) but substantially exceed recent trajectories in others (fidelity improvement, which tends to be logarithmic, not exponential).

**A more realistic projection** that respects physical constraints (error correction overhead, coherence scaling, fabrication yield) would place a CRQC closer to **2037–2043**, consistent with the BSI timeline and the more conservative end of the industry range, but inconsistent with the GRI median.

### 4.4 The "Error Correction Wall" Hypothesis

A central open question — unaddressed by any survey — is whether quantum error correction overhead exhibits a *phase transition* in practical resource requirements. Known results:

- Surface code overhead grows quadratically with code distance: physical qubits ≈ 2d² per logical qubit.
- Code distance d must grow logarithmically with total gate count to maintain fault tolerance.
- Total physical qubits for RSA-2048: O(n³ log n) in the surface code model.

If this scaling holds at all scales, a CRQC is an engineering problem with known resource requirements — a matter of scaling, not discovery. But if decoherence or correlated errors introduce *non-ideal scaling* at large code distances (e.g., d > 20–25), the resource requirements could grow super-polynomially, potentially rendering Shor's algorithm physically unrealizable at cryptographically relevant sizes.

**No survey asks respondents about this possibility.** Respondents are asked to estimate *when* RSA is broken, not *whether* the underlying error correction can scale indefinitely.

## 5. The Central Question: Do Surveys Acknowledge the Gap?

### 5.1 The FACTORING ∈ BQP ≠ FACTORING PRACTICAL Distinction

The consensus answer across all six survey traditions is **no** — none explicitly acknowledges the gap between "factoring is in BQP" and "factoring will be practical."

| Survey/Source | Asks About Algorithmic Possibility? | Asks About Physical Realizability? | Distinguishes BQP from Practice? |
|--------------|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| GRI / Mosca | ✗ | ✗ (bundled into single probability) | ✗ |
| NIST | ✗ (implicit: yes, hence migration) | ✗ | ✗ |
| NSA / CNSA 2.0 | ✗ (see hedging language) | Partial ("does not know when or even if") | Partial (implicit in "even if") |
| BSI | ✗ | ✓ (distinguishes physical vs. logical qubits) | Partial (technical differentiation) |
| ENISA | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Industry (Cloudflare, Google) | ✗ | Partial (explicit "decade+" caveats) | Partial |

**BSI comes closest** to the distinction by explicitly decomposing the threat into logical vs. physical qubit requirements. But even BSI does not ask whether the logical qubit requirements can be met at all — it provides a timeline for meeting them based on assumed progress curves.

**NSA's "even if" hedges** are the most epistemically honest public statement from any government agency. The phrase "does not know when or even if a quantum computer of sufficient size and power to exploit public key cryptography will exist" acknowledges that the threat is *conditional on unknown physical realizability*. But this hedging is not reflected in the CNSA 2.0 *timeline*, which mandates full deprecation by 2033 — the timeline presents certainty where the hedged language admits uncertainty.

### 5.2 Why This Gap Matters

If expert surveys systematically overestimate the probability of near-term quantum factoring:

1. **Resource allocation distortion.** Organizations may over-prioritize PQC migration at the expense of other security investments (e.g., zero-trust architectures, supply chain security, classical cryptographic hygiene).

2. **False certainty.** Published median estimates create a facade of expert consensus that pressures policymakers to set aggressive timelines without acknowledging the underlying uncertainty.

3. **Algorithmic monoculture risk.** The rush to standardize PQC has centered on lattice-based schemes. If lattice problems turn out to be easier than believed (classically or quantumly), the migration may create a single point of failure.

4. **Harvest-now risk is real, independent of timelines.** The shelf-life attack vector means that *even if a CRQC is 20+ years away*, data encrypted today with RSA is vulnerable if it must remain confidential beyond that horizon. This argument does not depend on optimistic hardware timelines and should be the primary driver of migration urgency — but it is often presented as secondary to the hardware arrival timeline.

## 6. Synthesis: A Unified Critique Framework

### 6.1 The Three Populations Model

The expert respondent pool can be modeled as three overlapping populations with different priors:

| Population | Prior | Typical Estimate | Representative Sources |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Hardware Determinists** | If factoring ∈ BQP, physical realization follows from continued investment | 2028–2032 | Quantum hardware researchers; some GRI respondents |
| **Conditional Optimists** | Physical realization is likely but timeline uncertain; error correction is the bottleneck | 2033–2040 | NIST, ENISA, industry practitioners |
| **Contingent Skeptics** | Physical realizability at scale is unknown; error correction may not scale; classical progress could intervene | 2040+ or "never" | Some cryptographers; complexity theorists; BSI (closest match) |

The GRI median (~2031) reflects a sampling distribution weighted toward Hardware Determinists and Conditional Optimists. The BSI timeline (~2040) reflects Conditional Optimist assumptions with explicit physical resource grounding. The NSA "even if" language acknowledges the Contingent Skeptic position but doesn't operationalize it.

### 6.2 What a Properly Specified Survey Would Ask

A more epistemically rigorous survey would decompose the question:

1. **Algorithmic:** Do you believe Shor's algorithm can be physically implemented at the scale required for RSA-2048 factoring? (Yes / Uncertain / No)
2. **Error Correction:** Do you believe quantum error correction can maintain logical error rates below ~10⁻¹⁵ per gate at code distances d ≥ 30? (Yes, with current approaches / Yes, with novel approaches / Uncertain / No)
3. **Timeline (conditional):** Conditional on (1) and (2) being achievable, in what year do you estimate RSA-2048 factoring becomes practical? (Point estimate + 90% confidence interval)
4. **Classical alternative:** Do you believe a classical polynomial-time factoring algorithm is: (Likely / Possible / Unlikely / Provably impossible)?
5. **Physical qubit scaling:** Do you believe millions of error-corrected physical qubits can be manufactured at sufficient yield? (Yes, via current approaches / Yes, via novel approaches / Uncertain / No)

No existing survey decomposes the question in this way. The result is that the published median combines algorithmic optimism, engineering optimism, and timeline optimism into a single, ambiguously interpretable number.

## 7. Recommendations

### 7.1 For This Research Program

The Phase 3 analysis of expert surveys supports the following conclusions relevant to the Shor Assumptions research program:

1. **Expert consensus is weaker than published medians suggest.** The IQR spans ~50 percentage points for near-term estimates. This is not "consensus" — it is structured disagreement.

2. **The BQP ≠ PRACTICAL gap is unexamined.** No survey distinguishes factoring's membership in BQP from its physical realizability. This is the central analytic gap that our research program addresses.

3. **Physical resource analysis (Phase 2) suggests timelines more conservative than the GRI median.** A CRQC before ~2037 appears inconsistent with current demonstrated error correction capacity and scaling trends, absent a breakthrough in error correction theory or physical qubit fidelity.

4. **The harvest-now-decrypt-later threat is the correct driver of migration urgency**, not the projected arrival date of a CRQC. This argument is timeline-independent and is robust to uncertainty about hardware realizability.

### 7.2 For Policy Communication

- Report interquartile ranges, not just medians.
- Distinguish between "factoring is in BQP" (proven) and "factoring will be practical" (speculative).
- Treat timelines as conditional on unknown physical assumptions, not as technological inevitabilities.
- Emphasize the shelf-life argument as the primary justification for PQC migration, not the projected arrival date of a CRQC.
