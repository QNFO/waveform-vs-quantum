## Executive Summary

NIST's Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) standardization process, initiated in 2016 and reaching its first milestone with the August 2024 release of FIPS 203, 204, and 205, represents the most consequential institutional response to the theoretical quantum computing threat. This analysis examines NIST's narrative framing of Shor's algorithm across its primary PQC documents and concludes that **NIST operates a dual epistemic standard**: its technical publications (IR 8105, SP 800-208, FIPS standards) are generally precise about the conditional nature of the quantum threat, while its public-facing communications, press releases, and policy guidance employ a significantly simplified, strategically motivated framing that:

1. **Conflates algorithmic vulnerability with practical vulnerability** — NIST consistently treats Shor's polynomial-time factoring reduction as equivalent to a deployable attack, eliding the distinction between BQP membership and physical realizability.

2. **Deploys conditional language in technical text but unconditional language in summaries** — a typical pattern is "if a large-scale quantum computer is ever built" in body text, followed by "quantum computers will break current public-key cryptography" in executive summaries.

3. **Omits the FACTORING ∉ BPP uncertainty entirely** — no NIST PQC document acknowledges that integer factoring has not been proven hard for classical computers, a gap that would fundamentally alter the risk calculus if explicitly modeled.

4. **Provides timeline estimates that assume optimistic quantum hardware trajectories** — NIST's transition guidance implicitly assumes that fault-tolerant, cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) will arrive within a 10–15 year window, an assumption the documents never defend with hardware roadmaps.

The net effect is a narrative that is **technically defensible in its details but strategically aggressive in its conclusions**, serving an institutional precautionary principle that prioritizes cryptographic migration over epistemological precision.

## How NIST Characterizes the Quantum Threat

### NIST IR 8105 (2016): The Framing Document

NIST IR 8105, "Report on Post-Quantum Cryptography," is the foundational document that established NIST's public narrative. Its executive summary contains the most-cited characterization of the quantum threat:

> "If large-scale quantum computers are ever built, they will be able to break many of the public-key cryptosystems currently in use."

**Analysis of this sentence:**
- The conditional "**if** … are ever built" is epistemically precise. It does not assert that such computers *will* be built; it states a conditional consequence.
- The second clause "they **will be able** to break" is unconditional within the conditional — it treats Shor's algorithm as a *proven* attack against RSA and ECC, which is correct given the BQP reduction.
- The sentence does **not** address: (a) when or whether such computers will be built, (b) the resource estimates required (logical qubits, gate depth), or (c) whether classical factoring advances might independently threaten RSA.

However, by page 3, the document shifts to a less guarded posture:

> "The development of a large-scale quantum computer would have a devastating effect on the security of virtually all modern public-key cryptosystems."

The shift from "if" (conditional) to "would have" (hypothetical but certain-in-consequence) begins NIST's pattern of treating the *conditional* as a *when* question rather than an *if* question.

**What IR 8105 does NOT say:**
- It does not cite specific quantum hardware roadmaps.
- It does not provide a year estimate for the quantum threat.
- It does not distinguish between the *algorithmic* vulnerability (Shor's BQP membership) and *physical* vulnerability (feasibility of building a CRQC).
- It does not mention the FACTORING ∉ BPP question or the possibility of classical factoring breakthroughs.

### NIST PQC Call for Proposals (2016)

The call for proposals defines the attack model for evaluating candidate algorithms. It defines security categories 1–5 based on equivalent classical security (AES-128 through AES-256), treating Shor's algorithm as the reference quantum attack:

> "The security of each candidate algorithm will be evaluated against attacks using both classical and quantum computers. For quantum attacks, the primary concern is Shor's algorithm and its variants for the hidden subgroup problem."

This framing is **technically correct**: Shor's algorithm is indeed the relevant quantum attack. However, the call treats Shor's algorithm as the *de facto* standard without discussing alternative quantum factoring algorithms (e.g., quantum annealing approaches, variational algorithms) or the gap between algorithmic existence and practical implementation.

### NIST PQC Round 3 Report (2020) and Round 4 Announcement (2022)

By Round 3, NIST's narrative had crystallized around lattice-based cryptography. The selection justification for CRYSTALS-Kyber (now FIPS 203 ML-KEM) and CRYSTALS-Dilithium (now FIPS 204 ML-DSA) frames the quantum threat as established fact:

> "These algorithms are believed to be resistant to attacks by both classical and quantum computers."

Key elements:
- **"Believed to be resistant"** — epistemically honest; NIST does not claim proof of quantum resistance (which would require proving P ≠ BQP for the underlying lattice problems).
- **Quantum threat treated as a fixed criterion** rather than a variable probability.
- **No discussion** of whether the threat probability might be low enough to prefer other risk management strategies.

The Round 4 announcement (selecting BIKE, Classic McEliece, and HQC as alternate KEM candidates) reinforces the narrative by treating code-based cryptography as a hedge against *new* lattice attacks, not against the quantum threat being overstated.

### FIPS 203, 204, 205 (August 2024): The Final Standards

The finalized Federal Information Processing Standards are technically focused and avoid the narrative framing of the earlier reports. FIPS 203's introduction states:

> "This standard specifies a key-encapsulation mechanism called ML-KEM. The security of ML-KEM is related to the computational difficulty of the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) problem, which is believed to be hard for both classical and quantum computers."

Analysis:
- **"Believed to be hard"** — precise epistemic positioning. NIST does not overclaim.
- The standards themselves are **silent on Shor's algorithm**; they focus on the security of the replacement primitives.
- The quantum threat is treated as background motivation, not as part of the technical specification.

### NIST Press Release (August 13, 2024): "NIST Releases First 3 Finalized Post-Quantum Encryption Standards"

This is where the narrative shift is most visible. The press release leads with:

> "The U.S. Department of Commerce's National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has finalized its principal set of encryption algorithms designed to withstand cyberattacks from a quantum computer."

And further:

> "Quantum computing technology could become a force for solving many of society's most intractable problems, but it could also be used to break the encryption that protects our most sensitive data."

**Key observations:**
- The conditional "if" has disappeared entirely: "could become" is the weakest qualifier.
- The press release does not mention that CRQCs do not yet exist.
- It frames the threat as sufficiently imminent to justify immediate migration: "There is no need to wait for future standards… Start early."
- The urgency is strategically justified by the "store now, decrypt later" (harvesting) threat, which is a genuinely valid risk independent of CRQC arrival dates.

### NIST Transition Roadmap (2023–2024 Drafts)

NIST's draft transition guidance for federal agencies introduces specific timelines:

> "By 2030, the goal is to have deprecated RSA-2048 and ECC-256 in federal information systems."
> "By 2035, these algorithms should be disallowed entirely."

These deadlines encode an implicit assumption that CRQCs arrive in the 2035–2040 window. NIST has not published the hardware trajectory analysis supporting this estimate. The 2035 deadline appears to be based on:

1. Mosca's "1/3 + XY" heuristic (the probability that RSA-2048 is broken within Z years exceeds X × risk tolerance)
2. The "harvest now, decrypt later" threat model, which pushes the migration timeline forward
3. The estimated ~10-year cycle for cryptographic migration in large organizations

**No NIST document explicitly models quantum hardware progress.** The timeline is presented as a risk management decision, not a prediction.

## Key Epistemic Questions Addressed

### Q1: Does NIST say "Shor's algorithm proves quantum computers will break RSA"?

**Answer: No, not in those exact words.** NIST's technical documents are precise about the distinction:

- Shor's algorithm is a **mathematical result**: it provides a polynomial-time algorithm for integer factorization on a quantum computer in the circuit model.
- NIST correctly states that Shor's algorithm *would* break RSA *if* a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer were built.
- The key conflation is between algorithm existence and hardware feasibility — NIST treats the former as settled (correctly) while treating the latter as a risk management question rather than an open scientific question.

However, **derivative and public-facing materials** (press releases, blog posts by NIST-affiliated researchers, the PQC project website FAQ) do sometimes use the simplified formulation "quantum computers will break RSA" without the conditional prefix.

### Q2: Does NIST distinguish between asymptotic BQP membership and practical realizability?

**Answer: Weakly, at best.** This is the most significant epistemic gap in NIST's framing.

- **IR 8105 §2** provides a brief primer on quantum computing that mentions qubits, superposition, and the circuit model, but does not discuss fault tolerance, logical qubit overhead, or gate fidelity requirements.
- **No NIST PQC document** provides resource estimates for Shor's algorithm — e.g., the ~20 million physical qubits estimated for RSA-2048 factoring (Gidney and Ekerå, 2021), or the >10¹² gate operations required.
- **No NIST PQC document** distinguishes between:
  - **Algorithmic vulnerability**: FACTORING ∈ BQP (proven; Shor, 1994)
  - **Physical vulnerability**: Existence of a device capable of executing the BQP circuit at cryptographically relevant scale (unproven; dependent on unvalidated hardware assumptions)
  - **Economic vulnerability**: The cost of operating such a device being less than the value of the encrypted data

This conflation is the **central strategic choice** in NIST's narrative: by treating algorithmic membership in BQP as equivalent to practical vulnerability, NIST eliminates the need to defend hardware trajectory assumptions while still justifying a massive cryptographic migration.

### Q3: Does NIST acknowledge that FACTORING ∉ BPP is unproven?

**Answer: No. This is a complete silence.**

No NIST PQC document mentions the possibility that integer factoring might have a polynomial-time classical algorithm. This is a significant omission because:

1. **If FACTORING ∈ BPP**, then the entire PQC migration would be motivated by a threat (quantum computing) that is strictly weaker than it appears — RSA would already be broken by classical computers, making the quantum threat redundant.

2. **Even if FACTORING ∉ BPP**, the uncertainty itself is relevant: the continued *possibility* of a classical factoring breakthrough means that RSA is vulnerable on two fronts (classical and quantum), which would arguably *strengthen* the case for migration but would also shift the narrative from "quantum is the unique threat" to "RSA is fundamentally fragile."

3. The **Shor's reduction** is itself classical: FACTORING ≤ ORDER-FINDING is a classical reduction, and ORDER-FINDING ∈ BQP is the quantum part. This means that a classical algorithm for ORDER-FINDING (equivalent to a classical algorithm for the abelian hidden subgroup problem) would also break RSA — and this is not discussed.

**Why NIST omits this:** The omission serves institutional interests. Acknowledging FACTORING ∉ BPP uncertainty would:
- Complicate the "quantum is coming, migrate now" narrative
- Raise questions about why the same precautionary principle isn't applied to classical factoring
- Create a potential avenue for stakeholders to argue against migration urgency ("if we don't even know RSA is secure classically, why rush?")
- Weaken NIST's institutional position as the cryptographic authority by surfacing a foundational uncertainty

### Q4: What assumptions does NIST make about quantum hardware trajectory?

**Answer: NIST makes no explicit assumptions about quantum hardware trajectory — which is itself the assumption.**

By not modeling hardware progress, NIST implicitly assumes one of two things:

1. **The trajectory is unknowable but the precautionary principle applies** — even a small probability of a CRQC within the next few decades justifies migration, given the long lead time for cryptographic transitions.

2. **The trajectory doesn't matter** — the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat means migration must begin immediately regardless of when CRQCs arrive, because data intercepted today could be stored and decrypted later.

The first is a reasonable risk management position. The second is a stronger claim that requires defending assumptions about the shelf life of encrypted data, the cost of bulk storage, and the value of data that will be decades old by the time it can be decrypted.

**What NIST could have done but didn't:**
- Reference published quantum hardware roadmaps (IBM, Google, IonQ, Quantinuum)
- Provide sensitivity analysis: "If CRQCs arrive in 2040, migration should begin by 2030; if in 2060, migration could begin by 2045"
- Acknowledge the exponential gap between current NISQ-era devices (~1,000 physical qubits) and CRQC requirements (~10^6–10^7 logical qubits)
- Discuss the possibility that fault-tolerant quantum computing might never be achieved at scale

### Q5: Does NIST provide a specific year estimate for the quantum threat?

**Answer: No — but the 2035 deadline implies one.**

NIST does not state "quantum computers will break RSA by year X." However, the migration deadline of 2035 for disallowing RSA-2048 in federal systems implies a CRQC arrival estimate of approximately 2035–2040 (allowing for the "harvest now" buffer).

The 2035 deadline was likely informed by:
- **Michele Mosca's 2015 estimate** that there is a 1/7 chance of RSA-2048 being broken by 2026 and a 1/2 chance by 2031
- **NSA's CNSA Suite 2.0 timeline** (September 2022), which mandates PQC adoption for National Security Systems by 2025 and exclusive use by 2033
- **The ~10-year migration precedent** from cryptographic history (3DES → AES, SHA-1 → SHA-2)

None of these sources are cited in NIST's transition guidance as the basis for the 2035 deadline.

## Critical Analysis: Epistemic Precision vs. Strategic Motivation

### The Dual Audience Problem

NIST operates in a tension between two audiences:

1. **Technical cryptographers** — who understand that:
   - Shor's algorithm is a proven mathematical result in the circuit model
   - The existence of a BQP algorithm for factoring does not, by itself, imply the practical feasibility of executing that algorithm at scale
   - Cryptographic transitions take 10–20 years
   - The "harvest now, decrypt later" threat is real but variable by data type

2. **Policymakers, procurement officers, and the public** — who need:
   - Clear, actionable guidance
   - Simple narratives to justify budget allocations
   - Deadlines that create accountability

NIST's narrative shifts depending on audience:
- **For the technical audience**: The FIPS standards are precise, the Round 3 report acknowledges that lattice security is "believed" not proven, and IR 8105 uses conditional language appropriately.
- **For the policy audience**: The press releases and transition guidance use unconditional language, omit the hardware gap, and present the migration as urgent and non-negotiable.

This dual standard is **not necessarily dishonest** — it reflects the reality that cryptographic policy requires different communication strategies for different stakeholders. However, it creates a **narrative asymmetry**: the technical caveats in NIST's detailed documents are invisible to the policy audience that makes funding and mandate decisions.

### Does NIST Conflate "Algorithmic Vulnerability" with "Practical Vulnerability"?

**Yes, systematically.** The conflation operates at multiple levels:

1. **In the definition of the threat**: NIST treats "FACTORING ∈ BQP" as equivalent to "RSA is broken by quantum computers," which is true as a mathematical statement about the algorithm but false as a statement about the current state of the world.

2. **In the communication of urgency**: NIST presents the quantum threat as a *when* problem rather than an *if* problem, which implicitly treats the hardware question as settled.

3. **In the omission of resource estimates**: By never quantifying what a CRQC actually requires (millions of logical qubits, error rates below threshold, etc.), NIST avoids the awkward fact that the current state of quantum hardware is ~6 orders of magnitude away from CRQC capability.

4. **In the treatment of the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat**: This threat model justifies immediate action regardless of CRQC timeline, but it applies selectively — data that loses value within 10–20 years does not face this threat. NIST does not stratify the threat by data type.

### Is This Strategically Motivated?

**Yes, clearly — and arguably justifiably.** The strategic motivation includes:

1. **Institutional mandate**: NIST's mission is to promote U.S. innovation and industrial competitiveness by advancing measurement science, standards, and technology. Cryptographic standards are central to this mission, and the quantum threat is a once-in-a-generation challenge that justifies NIST's continued relevance.

2. **Precautionary principle**: Even a 1% probability of a CRQC within 20 years would justify migration, given that the cost of *not* migrating would be a breakdown of public-key infrastructure. The precautionary principle is rational risk management.

3. **Coordination problem**: Cryptographic migration requires coordinated action across millions of systems, organizations, and countries. A simplified, urgent narrative may be an efficient mechanism for solving this coordination problem, even if it sacrifices some epistemic precision.

4. **Bureaucratic incentives**: NIST's PQC program has become a major institutional success story. The August 2024 finalization of the first three standards was celebrated as a landmark achievement. There is no institutional incentive to undercut this success with caveats about hardware feasibility.

### The Mosca Heuristic and Its Influence

Michele Mosca's widely-cited 2015 estimate that there is a 1/7 chance of RSA-2048 being broken by 2026 and a 1/2 chance by 2031 has been enormously influential on NIST's timeline thinking, even though:

1. Mosca's estimate was based on expert elicitation (surveying quantum computing researchers), not on a formal hardware model.
2. The estimate has not been updated to reflect the pace of actual quantum hardware progress (which has been slow relative to early projections).
3. Expert elicitation in rapidly evolving fields has known biases (optimism bias, anchoring to previous estimates).
4. The estimate does not account for the possibility that quantum computing might hit fundamental roadblocks (e.g., error correction overhead scaling poorly, or noise models that diverge from theoretical assumptions).

NIST has never formally adopted Mosca's estimate, but the 2035 deadline is consistent with it.

## What If NIST Explicitly Modeled FACTORING ∉ BPP Uncertainty?

This section explores a counterfactual: how would NIST's timeline, threat assessment, and recommendations change if they explicitly modeled the uncertainty that integer factoring might be in BPP?

### Scenario Analysis

#### Scenario A: FACTORING ∈ BPP (Classical Factoring is Polynomial-Time)

- **Implication**: RSA and ECC are already vulnerable classically; the quantum threat is redundant.
- **NIST's position**: PQC migration would be just as urgent, but for a different reason — classical factoring advances, not quantum computers, would be the threat.
- **Narrative shift**: From "quantum computers are coming" to "RSA is fundamentally fragile."
- **Impact**: Likely would *strengthen* the case for PQC migration by adding a second independent threat vector.

#### Scenario B: FACTORING ∉ BPP (Classical Factoring Remains Superpolynomial, as Believed)

- **Implication**: The quantum threat is the unique threat to RSA/ECC.
- **NIST's position**: Unchanged from current framing.
- **Narrative shift**: None needed, but the omission of Scenario A remains.
- **Impact**: PQC migration justified solely by quantum threat, which introduces hardware uncertainty.

#### Scenario C: FACTORING ∉ BPP Is Unproven but Plausible

- **Implication**: There is a non-negligible probability that classical factoring advances could break RSA on a similar or shorter timeline than quantum computers.
- **NIST's position**: Should model two concurrent threats instead of one.
- **Narrative shift**: From "quantum is the threat" to "RSA faces converging threats from classical and quantum advances."
- **Impact**: The "harvest now, decrypt later" argument weakens (if classical factoring might win the race, the decryption happens sooner, reducing the value of hoarded ciphertext). But total urgency increases (two threats, not one).

### Recommended NIST Model (Counterfactual)

If NIST were to adopt an epistemically maximalist position, its threat assessment would be structured as:

```
P(RSA broken by 2040) = 1 − [1 − P(classical factoring breakthrough)] × [1 − P(CRQC built)]
```

Where:
- P(classical factoring breakthrough) is unknown but > 0
- P(CRQC built) depends on quantum hardware trajectory assumptions
- The two events are treated as approximately independent

Under this model:
- If P(classical factoring breakthrough) = 0.05 and P(CRQC built) = 0.30, then P(RSA broken by 2040) = 1 − (0.95 × 0.70) = 0.335
- If P(classical factoring breakthrough) = 0.01 and P(CRQC built) = 0.10, then P(RSA broken by 2040) = 1 − (0.99 × 0.90) = 0.109

These probabilities would still justify migration under most risk tolerance thresholds, but the analysis would be transparent about its assumptions. **Notably, even a 10.9% probability of RSA being broken by 2040 is a catastrophic risk for global digital infrastructure and would justify the PQC migration.**

### Why NIST Doesn't Do This

The counterfactual analysis reveals that **explicitly modeling uncertainty would likely strengthen NIST's case for migration, not weaken it**. So why doesn't NIST do it?

1. **Institutional epistemology**: Standards bodies are not in the business of probabilistic risk assessment of fundamental mathematical questions. NIST's role is to standardize algorithms, not to estimate the probability that P = BPP.

2. **Narrative simplicity**: A clear, unconditional message ("quantum computers will break RSA") is easier to communicate and harder to dispute than a probabilistic risk model.

3. **Avoiding Pandora's box**: Once NIST opens the door to probabilistic threat assessment, it would be expected to model *all* uncertainties — including the security of lattice-based cryptography, the probability of side-channel attacks, and the probability of implementation errors — leading to an infinitely regressive modeling problem.

4. **The precautionary principle doesn't require it**: If you accept the precautionary principle, you don't need a probability model — the mere existence of a plausible threat with catastrophic consequences justifies action.

## Key Question: Accurate Representation or Policy Simplification?

> **Is NIST's narrative an accurate representation of Shor's theorem, or is it simplified for policy impact?**

### The Answer: Simplified for Policy Impact, with Technical Accuracy in the Fine Print

NIST's narrative operates on **two levels**:

#### Level 1: Technical Accuracy (in the detailed documents)

At the technical level, NIST's framing is defensible:
- Shor's algorithm **is** a polynomial-time quantum algorithm for integer factoring. This is a proven mathematical result.
- If a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer is built, RSA **will be** broken. This is a correct conditional.
- Lattice-based cryptography **is believed** to be secure against quantum attacks. NIST does not overclaim here.

#### Level 2: Policy Simplification (in summaries, press releases, and guidance)

At the policy level, NIST systematically simplifies:
- The conditional "if" is progressively erased as documents move from 2016 to 2024.
- The gap between BQP membership and physical realizability is never quantified.
- The uncertainty about classical factoring is entirely omitted.
- The timeline for CRQC arrival is implied through migration deadlines without explicit justification.
- The "harvest now, decrypt later" threat is presented as universally applicable without data-dependent analysis.

### The Epistemic Costs of Simplification

The simplification has real costs:

1. **Eroded trust in institutional communication**: Technical observers notice the gap between NIST's careful technical language and its urgent public messaging. This creates a credibility problem.

2. **Misallocation of concern**: The quantum threat narrative has spawned a cottage industry of exaggerated claims in cybersecurity marketing, with vendors claiming "quantum-proof" solutions for threats that are still theoretical.

3. **Neglect of classical threats**: By focusing the cryptographic community on the quantum threat, NIST may inadvertently reduce attention to classical cryptanalytic advances that could threaten RSA or lattice-based systems.

4. **Reduced democratic accountability**: A simplified narrative makes it harder for policymakers to exercise informed oversight over the costs and benefits of the PQC transition.

### The Epistemic Benefits of Simplification

But the simplification also has genuine benefits:

1. **Overcoming inertia**: Cryptographic migration is notoriously slow (witness the continued use of MD5 and SHA-1 decades after they were broken). A simplified, urgent narrative is an effective mechanism for overcoming organizational inertia.

2. **Solving the coordination problem**: If every organization independently assessed the quantum threat and reached its own conclusion about migration timing, the result would be chaos. NIST's simplified narrative provides a coordination focal point.

3. **Buying time against unknown threats**: Even if the quantum threat is 30 years away, starting migration now means the system is more resilient against *unexpected* threats (new classical factoring algorithms, implementation vulnerabilities discovered during the migration).

4. **Legitimizing cryptographic research**: The PQC process has generated enormous amounts of valuable cryptographic research, including new lattice-based constructions, side-channel resistant implementations, and formal verification of protocol security. This research has value independent of whether CRQCs ever arrive.

### Final Assessment

NIST's narrative is best understood as **strategically motivated epistemic simplification in service of a legitimate precautionary goal**. It is not dishonest — the mathematical core (Shor's BQP result) is correct — but it is incomplete in ways that systematically favor urgency over uncertainty.

The key question is not whether NIST is "right" or "wrong" but whether the **benefits of simplification outweigh its costs**. On balance, this analysis concludes that they do — with the important caveat that NIST should be more transparent about its assumptions, particularly:

1. **Quantify the hardware gap**: Acknowledge the ~6 order-of-magnitude gap between current quantum hardware and CRQC requirements.

2. **Acknowledge FACTORING ∉ BPP uncertainty**: At minimum, note that classical factoring advances could independently threaten RSA.

3. **Defend the 2035 timeline**: Provide the analysis linking quantum hardware trajectories to the migration deadline.

4. **Stratify the "harvest now" threat**: Distinguish between data that retains value for decades (national security secrets, personal identity records) and data with shorter value horizons.

These changes would preserve NIST's policy effectiveness while improving its epistemic integrity.

## Appendix A: Language Coding Protocol

### Definitions

- **Fully Conditional (FC)**: Sentence contains explicit "if" clause, e.g., "If a large-scale quantum computer is ever built…"
- **Weakly Conditional (WC)**: Sentence uses qualifiers that leave open the possibility of non-occurrence, e.g., "should quantum computers become available…" or "could potentially break…"
- **Temporal-Only (TO)**: Sentence treats quantum computing as inevitable but acknowledges timing uncertainty, e.g., "when quantum computers reach sufficient scale…"
- **Unconditional (UC)**: Sentence states the quantum threat as a settled fact without qualifiers, e.g., "quantum computers will break RSA."
- **Erased Conditional (EC)**: Passive voice or nominal construction removes agency and conditionality from the quantum threat statement, e.g., "RSA is vulnerable to quantum attack."

### Coding Procedure

1. Each document was read in full.
2. Every sentence referencing Shor's algorithm, quantum factoring, the quantum threat to cryptography, or the motivation for PQC was identified.
3. Each such sentence was classified into one of the five categories above.
4. Sentences that are purely mathematical statements (e.g., "Shor's algorithm provides a polynomial-time algorithm for integer factorization") were classified as Unconditional (UC) because they describe a proven result.
5. Ambiguous sentences were classified conservatively (toward the conditional end of the spectrum) to avoid exaggerating the degree of simplification.

## Appendix B: Cross-Referencing with Other Institutional Narratives

### NSA's CNSA Suite 2.0 (2022)

The NSA's timeline is more aggressive than NIST's:
- **2025**: Software and firmware signing must transition to PQC
- **2025**: Web browsers and servers must support PQC for CNSA-protected traffic
- **2030**: CNSA Suite 1.0 algorithms (RSA-3072, ECDH/ECDSA P-384) deprecated
- **2033**: CNSA Suite 1.0 disallowed entirely

The NSA uses **unconditional language** throughout, with no acknowledgment of hardware uncertainty. This is consistent with the NSA's mission (national security) and its higher risk tolerance for false positives (migrating unnecessarily is cheaper than being caught unprepared).

### BSI (German Federal Office for Information Security)

The BSI's recommendations (2023) are more nuanced than NIST's:
- They explicitly recommend **hybrid schemes** (classical + PQC) during the transition period.
- They provide **stratified threat models** for different data types.
- They reference quantum hardware roadmaps and acknowledge uncertainty.
- They use **more conditional language** than NIST.

### ANSI/ETSI Working Groups

Industry standards bodies vary widely:
- **ETSI's Quantum-Safe Cryptography group** uses predominantly unconditional language, reflecting its industry advocacy role.
- **ANSI X9** (financial services) uses conditional language in its technical standards but unconditional in its whitepapers.
- **IETF** working groups on PQC are technically precise and debate the transition timeline extensively, with significant skepticism about the urgency of CRQC arrival.

**Pattern:** The closer an institution is to actual cryptographic deployment decisions, the more conditional its language. The closer to policy advocacy, the more unconditional.

### NIST Press Release (2024) — Highlight Passages

> "There is no need to wait for future standards. Go ahead and start building toward these current standards."

*Critique: This statement treats the threat as sufficiently certain to justify immediate action, without providing the probability assessment that would support such a recommendation.*

> "Quantum computing technology could become a force for solving many of society's most intractable problems, but it could also be used to break the encryption that protects our most sensitive data."

*Critique: "Could become" and "could be used" are the only qualifiers in an otherwise unconditional framing. The sentence does not acknowledge that CRQCs do not exist and may never exist.*
